Factors that contribute to risk profile

The biggest contributor to risk profile is age but there are several other factors that contribute in risk profile. Risk profile helps you identify your asset allocation.

1. Age

The younger you are, the more risks you can take. If you are just 25 years old and have no debt, no dependents etc you can take much more risk than someone who is 50 years old and has 2 children and a house loan to pay off. Hence you can invest more in equities as your investment horizon is longer.

2. Income & Debt

More income and less debt = more risks. If your income allows you to save 20-30% of your money every month, consider yourself lucky. If you have large debt that you are paying off then you will be hesitant to invest a large portion of your investments in equities.

3. Dependents

More dependents = less risky investments. If you are supporting your childrenâ€™s education or your parents healthcare or 100s of other expenses that can be there for your dependents, then you would not want to invest a large part of your investments in risky assets.

4. Assets

If you already own a house and are not paying any home loan EMIs, kudos! If you own a lot of Jewlry or stocks or bonds etc that count as assets, then you are more financially secure than someone who does not own any assets. More assets you have, more risks you can take. That doesnt mean you should go ahead and buy risky stocks, but it is an indicator that you are more financially comfortable and can afford to take risks.

5. Savings/ Emergency funds

If you already have 6 months of savings in the bank, you will feel more comfortable taking risks and investing in riskier instruments. However if you have no liquid savings and you end up buying some small cap stocks that fall 50%, you will be trapped! Never make the mistake of investing in stocks before you have your emergency fund covered.

6. Risk Aversion

You might have the best of all of the above factors but you are a risk averse person in general. In that case you should invest less in risky instruments like direct equities and more in SIPs of mutual funds or debt/FDs.
